# The Nth Gambit

*A Star Wars fan fiction draft*

## Opening Crawl

*A long time ago in a galaxy far, far away....*

**THE NTH GAMBIT**

The GALACTIC CIVIL WAR has reached its bloodiest hour. Across a thousand systems the fleets of the EMPIRE and the REBEL ALLIANCE grind one another to ruin, and still no victor emerges.

Desperate to end the slaughter, LUKE SKYWALKER has struck a bargain with the Emperor: the war will be decided not by arms, but by a single game, played beneath the dome of the Senate itself. The loser will lay down its weapons forever.

To keep the Force from the field, the diminished TRADE FEDERATION has lined the chamber with creatures that still all who would wield it. Now Jedi and Sith — only those few not away at the front — must meet as mere players. But in striking his wager, young Skywalker has wagered more than a galaxy. He has staked his own soul, and the bottom of the ninth is drawing near....

---

The diamond was chalked onto the floor of the Senate rotunda, and the chalk was the strange part, though no one in the stands would have thought to look.

It wasn't chalk. The calcified, marble-white stuff they etched the baselines with came from exactly one place in the galaxy: a single undersea volcano, venting on a world whose name had long since been left behind by the shifting trade routes. The routes wandered like a sandy river, forever cutting some new bed — they'd get you from the same point A to the same point B, more or less, but the systems strung along the way just quietly fell out of mind. Every so often one of them did something that mattered and earned a bump of trans-galactic traffic, and the hyperspace channels would shift a little nearer, on average, to whatever world was having its moment. This one hadn't had a moment in a very long time.

And what it vented had the one property no other organic material in the galaxy could claim: midichlorians clung to it. Not to the living thing that had grown the shell, but to the shell itself, long after the life had gone out of it. The only dead matter in creation the Force would not let go of.

So you could say, if you're into that kind of thing, that the baselines were painted with the Force. But that's torturing the idea a little bit. Force lines: one running out to first, one falling away down from third. The fair from the foul, drawn in the only substance left that still remembered being alive.

Luke couldn't see them. Not the way he should have. They were bright paint on old stone to him now, nothing more — white lines laid across the great seal of the Republic that the Empire had never bothered to scrape away. He felt a little dizzy looking at them. It probably wasn't real dizziness; more likely just a perceptual glitch left over from the ysalamiri spa treatment they'd run him through on arrival — the slow, total foreclosure of a sense he'd spent years learning to lean on. Like standing up too fast inside a body that no longer answered all the way to the edges.

Luke stood at the mouth of the visitors' dugout and reached out with his senses. The leather of a dozen different domestic animals mixed with the smell of the vast diaspora packed into the chamber — sweat and rain-damp cloaks and the dust of systems that no longer existed. The Senate had been thrown open to the public for this. Citizens and refugees and the merely curious, come to watch the epic conclusion of the war unfold in front of them. The fate of a thousand worlds, turning on a single apex moment.

If you had told Luke, that first night in the desert, that the final battle would be fought this way — no fleets, no trench run, just nine innings and a bat cut from a living moon — he'd have laughed. He wasn't laughing now. He wasn't feeling much of anything.

The Trade Federation had been obsequious enough lately — in the aftermath of some embarrassing losses, a significant number of them had been stripped of their fiduciary privileges — that arbitrating the game fell to them almost by default. Those stripped delegates had become an unofficial guild of frontier judiciaries, going world to world trying to coax the galaxy back toward the level of consumerism the Federation forecasts as its own inflection point — the threshold past which it rebounds. And the Federation, having "inherited" the late Grand Admiral's ysalamiri breeding moon, had lined the Senate chamber with the creatures that day. No ordinary Force user could extend his reach past a few centimeters of his own skin; even the strongest, straining at the limit, could push it no further than a handsbreadth. Imagine it: meeting the Sith Lord in single combat, with only our own physical bodies and the speed of our minds to bear down on the other.

"I wonder who's stronger," thought Luke. "Me, or Vader?"

---

That same moon had done more for the Federation than shade one ballgame. With its creatures in hand it was no longer quite defenceless against the Jedi or the Sith — but it had its mission to mind, and it seemed content, now, to travel different routes than the rest of the galaxy. It didn't keep to itself, exactly. People simply stopped seeking it out. Its delegates wandered the edges of charted space like a mendicant order — a strange thing to find in a galaxy that already had its Jedi and its Sith: a new monastic order, fallen out the bottom of a diminished Federation, the houses that had drifted below the cutoff line, too near the epicenter when the crash came.

There were still grand families, to be sure. But the schisms had fractured the Federation's governance into a handful of sects, and there were six Viceroys now. Even the Arbitrator sect had a Viceroy, if you could believe it. They remained a great power in a galaxy of obscene disparities — of wealth, and of distance, for so many of them had simply dissolved into the spaces between the stars, out where the systems were too undeveloped for anyone to have bothered plotting them: no charts, only a curve of gravimetric potential that hinted there might be planets. And where there were planets, there were moons. Forested ones. And what the galaxy needed a great deal of, just now, was cellulose.

---

No one seemed to mind. The Federation was bringing real resources into devastated systems, and there were a great many people living under cloth roofs these days. Cellulose structures would be critical to the recovery of a hundred worlds — and the Federation had been acquiring forest moons to supply them, moon after moon. No one saw it come. All at once they simply *had* so many. But the galaxy was littered with the things.

What the Federation had actually cracked was the harder problem: how to chain together the countless micro-hops it takes to go looking for something you don't already know is there. A hyperdrive earns its keep on the long hauls — the known routes, the ones that drop you somewhere with a spaceport and a yard that can service the drive when it's spent. To re-tool that same power plant to feel its way along corridors nobody travels, hop after hop after hop on a single charge, was a different art entirely. Like ice-boarding blindfolded.

I wouldn't want to live their lives. But you have to respect what they've pulled off — and if you don't, if you let yourself forget them out there in the dark, they'll be back to full power somewhere in our future before we've even noticed. We'll have forgotten they were ever hurt at all. They won't have. The Federation does not forget an embarrassment.

Luke had learned to enjoy that, in the short while they'd spent hiding in the ice caves of a frozen moon. So few would ever think to run to an ice world when there were so many forest moons to choose from. Even the Empire had built its Death Star in orbit of one — you couldn't afford to disregard what a forest moon offered that an ice moon never could. Which was exactly why the ice moon had kept them hidden as long as it did. It was a heck of a time together, however short. It was always going to end. And when the first walkers showed on our scans, we all just kind of sensed it: that from there on, we were fighting to give our friends and families the time to get away.

Another forest moon gone to some use no one would remember, he thought. The galaxy had too many of them. You could lay their survey photos side by side and not tell one from the next — green on green on green, the same miracle copied out a billion times.

It was the rock-and-ice worlds he wondered about. The ones with no forest, no canopy breathing into the dark. He suspected they never got far — that they burned themselves down before anyone thought to come look, because nobody was looking. Hyperspace wasn't for that. You jumped to where the business was, where the culture was, the lit and crowded places that already knew their own names; you didn't jump somewhere just to see what was there. If you actually wanted to *explore* — to wander, to stumble onto things — you'd be better off with wind and a set of sails than with the fastest drive ever built. So most of the galaxy went unwitnessed: worlds on fire, burning in the light of a bare rock moon, while the green ones got all the visitors and grew all the bats.

A smuggler who claimed he'd played the game on some moon Luke had never heard of. Two pilots. A protocol droid insisting, against all evidence, that his command of the rules was essential to their chances. *Could be useful*, Luke thought quietly — but he had the sense that somehow this game wasn't going to end on a technicality. Lives would be lost today. Friends, maybe. Maybe even a protocol droid. And himself — the only one among them who could feel the cold the Emperor was pouring into the room like water filling a hold.

Across the field, Vader took practice swings. Slow. Patient. The bat looked like a toothpick in his fist and he held it as though it might also be a saber, as though the distinction were merely a matter of agreement.

"You understand the terms," the Emperor said. He had not raised his voice and yet it arrived at Luke's ear from across the entire rotunda. "Nine innings. The Alliance lays down its arms, or the Empire does. The galaxy will honor the outcome." A thin smile. "I always honor a wager, young Skywalker. It is the rest of you who break your word."

Luke said nothing. He'd known, signing this, that the Emperor would not have agreed unless he saw a way to win something larger than a war. The fleet at the front would hold the line for nine innings. What he hadn't known — what he was only now beginning to feel, watching his own hands tighten on the bat — was which side of the diamond his anger wanted him standing on by the bottom of the ninth.

---

It was not that the wood was rare. Cellulose was the commonest miracle in the galaxy — every canopy moon spun it out by the hectare, breathing green into the dark. But each world had grown its own species in its own long solitude, and only a handful had ever produced the grain that mattered: dense and yielding at once, springing back the instant before it broke, the kind of wood that turned a hard swing into a long arc and put a ball over a wall a master could not have reached by strength alone. Mummra's Reach. Old Caldee. Greenfall. You could count the bat-moons on one hand and have fingers to spare, out of all the numberless forests in all the tended systems.

Which was a kind of mercy, Luke thought, because the bats were never the scarce thing. The masters were.

Tonight the room should have been brimming with them — every great hand the game had ever produced, the swing and the grip passed down across centuries. Instead the rotunda held a smuggler, two pilots, and a droid. The rest were out there in the conflagration, scattered across the mass migrations and the hardship spread through aligned and unaligned systems alike, holding a line that did not know a wager was being laid on its behalf. The galaxy had wood enough for ten thousand games. It had run almost entirely out of the people who knew what to do with it.

---

Greenfall, they called it on the manifests, when they bothered to call it anything at all. Worlds wrapped in canopy, oxygen pouring out into the dark as though some gardener had gone through the systems pressing seeds into every habitable shadow.

Han had a theory. Han always had a theory. "Somebody planted 'em," he said, oiling the grip. "Whole galaxy's a garden somebody walked off and left. You ever see a system that *wasn't* tended? Moons of bare rock. Moons of ammonia so cold it'd peel you down to the bone before you finished screaming." He turned the bat in the light. Pale, close-grained, cut from a tree that had grown for two hundred years on a moon nobody could find on purpose. "This came off a *living* place. You can feel the difference. Even the Emperor can feel the difference, which is why he hates holding one."

Luke took the bat. It was warm, the way wood is warm and metal never is — and faint beneath the warmth, the last shimmers of a life two hundred years in the growing, not quite gone out of it. Strange, that he could feel even that. It was the least connected to the living things around him he had ever been. Curious. Quiet.

His reach was simply gone. What the lizards in the rafters had thrown over him was a shell of pure absence — a handsbreadth out from his skin, uniform, following every motion, like some flight suit cut from nothing and fitted so close it moved when he moved. It let his Force sense no farther than a half-step in any direction. No user could feel another now. Two of them could pass within arm's reach and never know it; only if they collided would each catch, for an instant, the shimmer of the other's suit. Inside his own, the warm bat was the only living thing left to him.

And he thought: a saber takes a life. This gave one back, as a thing you swing toward home.

Maybe that was the whole gambit. Make the old man hold something that had been *alive*.
