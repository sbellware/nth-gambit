# The Nth Gambit — Storyline

*Proposed structures that tie the universe notes together into a plot. Companion to the Manuscript and Universe files.*

## The spine

**One line:** To end a galaxy-spanning war, Luke wagers everything on a single baseball game where the Force has been switched off — and the only way to *win* is to free the tormented creatures doing the switching, a mercy that can only be performed by the one broken man nobody recognizes as a Jedi.

**Theme:** the exploited held in thrall by zealots serving the already-gone — and the Force, at the very end, recognizing itself. Nearly every central figure is a broken or enslaved thing: the ysalamiri, Ryn, the clones, even Vader. The story frees each through recognition and *contact*, set against two architects of domination who are themselves already, in their way, dead — the Emperor (who cannot bear to hold a living thing) and Thrawn (literally late, his galaxy-remaking scheme inherited by fools chasing cellulose).

## The braid — three strands

- **Strand A — The Game (Senate, present day).** Close on Luke and the dugout. The wager, the stilling, the inning-by-inning tactics, hold-the-tie, the hidden bat-conduit, the extra innings, the twelfth-inning duel. This is the clock and the spectacle, and the high-concept hook.
- **Strand B — The Investigation (off-world, present day).** Close on Ryn, intercut. Run by his handler **Val Korr** over comms, he tracks down Thrawn's breeding-moon science: what the ysalamiri are, what was done to them, and how to switch them off. Carries the mystery and Ryn's interiority — the visions, the buried Force he doesn't know he has.
- **Strand C — Flashbacks.** Two veins that turn out to be one place: (1) wartime ball played at the front, which explains how this motley roster knows the game and lets the war's cost land obliquely; and (2) Ryn's abandonment on Thrawn's breeding moon with his true-believer sergeant. The reveal that these are the *same moon* — that Ryn suffered there alongside the ysalamiri — is the hinge of the whole book.

## Sequence / beat map

1. **Open** on the diamond: the coral dust, the stilling, the wager. Establish the game and the impossible constraint. *(A)*
2. **Early innings** — the suit-feeling, reach tiering, secrecy-as-scarcity, the bat's pins-and-needles tingle planted as innocent sensation. First intercuts of Ryn off-world: a broken vet on a job he only half-understands, Val Korr's voice in his ear, his visions dismissed as damage. Front-line ball flashbacks begin. *(A / B / C)*
3. **Middle innings** — the investigation deepens. The ysalamiri are *alive* and suffering; the jellyfish heritage; the breeding-state truth; Thrawn's records surface (the envy, the failed extermination, the pivot to *breaking* them). The dugout learns the real win is to free the creatures, and the obstacle is communication. Hold-the-tie becomes the conscious strategy — a secret clock only their side is watching. *(B feeding A)*
4. **The moon flashbacks converge** with the investigation: Ryn was garrisoned and abandoned on Thrawn's breeding moon, kept there by the fanatic sergeant. He and the ysalamiri did their ruin on the same poisoned rock, at the same time. The buried-Force clues accrue — he's the one player the stilling can't touch; inert midichlorians, like the baselines. *(C → B)*
5. **The tied eighth** — the protocol droid (C-3PO) points out a *final battle* can't end in a draw. Into extras; the stilling visibly thins. *(A)*
6. **The convergence (the twelfth)** — Ryn is brought in and substituted onto the team. The hidden-ball play produces the tag; the arbiter moves to call it; Vader ignites his bat to behead the ref, then rips up and lights the baselines as Force-lances. The duel: bats, lances, and the wordless *turn* when forced contact makes Vader feel the injury his own takeout slide left in his son. **Simultaneously:** Ryn breaks free of his trauma, reaches the ysalamiri, hands them the legacy Thrawn's farming erased; they free him in return; the stilling collapses. **The mercy and the killing in the same few minutes.** The Emperor's slow, intimate death. *(A + B converge)*
7. **Epilogue** — Ryn and Lryn in the pod; Luke's unplayed holo-invitation to the Jedi school; Threepio waiting on an answer; the ysalamiri "friends." The choice left open. *(coda)*

## How the two climaxes interlock

- Ryn's liberation is the gentle collapse of the stilling; Vader's lances are the violent one. Reconcile them as two pressures on the same failing cage — Ryn's the one that actually ends it, Vader's the spectacle melting it from the other side.
- **The rhyme:** at the exact moment Luke performs the intimate *killing* of the Emperor, Ryn performs the intimate *mercy* of the ysalamiri. The book's two hands close at once.
- **The Force "recognizing itself" happens twice:** Vader feels his son through contact and turns; Ryn and the ysalamiri recognize each other and free each other. Both are the precise rebuke to Thrawn's project to abolish the Force — the thing he bred to silence the Force is undone by the Force seeing itself.

## Key decisions to lock (with recommendations)

1. **POV — LOCKED.** Dual close-third: Luke for the game, Ryn for the investigation, with periodic short dips into a ysalamiri's alien interior, and Han / the droid for texture. **No Thrawn POV** — see decision 4: his voice is never attempted, in the present or in flashback.
2. **When the reader learns Ryn is a Force user — LOCKED: kept hidden.** His being "unaffected" reads as luck or brokenness; the prose only *wonders*, so the late "he was one of the Force's own all along" turn lands. **The visions are the engine:** Ryn's PTSD visions turn out to be *Force* visions — at least some of them — and he can never tell which is which: trauma hallucination or true sight. The ambiguity is permanent, and it's part of his torment. **The dark turn at the end:** Luke wants to train him — but Luke is haunted by the precedent of Obi-Wan's naivete in training the broken, traumatized **Anakin Skywalker**, the very man Luke has just helped pull back from the dark. Offering the broken and powerful a road to the Force is *exactly* how Vader was made. So the Jedi-school invitation isn't pure hope; it carries dread — and it turns the epilogue's unanswered question into something genuinely fraught, for Luke as much as for Ryn.
3. **Timeline** — *Recommend* a single afternoon (the game) intercut with a compressed off-world investigation that's been racing toward this day, plus flashbacks — the investigation must deliver its answer *just* in time for the twelfth, a ticking clock against the innings.
4. **Thrawn's presence — LOCKED: dead and discovered, never voiced.** He appears only as records and annotations Ryn digs up — cold research with the vanity showing through — never on-page, *not even in memory or flashback.* **Principle:** no one should write Thrawn's voice but Timothy Zahn, so the book never attempts it. We get the *evidence* of Thrawn — the farm, the science, the marginalia — and let the reader assemble the man, but his interiority and his speech stay entirely off the page.
5. **The midichlorian-death lore — LOCKED: narrator musing only.** Keep it as the prose *wondering* ("do they gather here to die?"), never confirmed — like the Emperor's vulnerability. The reader feels the weight of the idea without the book ever asserting it as fact.

## Alternate shapes (if you want a different build)

- **A — Game-pure (novella).** Never leave the Senate. The moon, Thrawn, and Ryn's findings arrive entirely as comms and flashback. Tighter, more claustrophobic, more unified; the off-world world exists only as voices and reports. Strong for a shorter, sharper book.
- **B — Investigation-forward.** Open cold on Ryn on the moon; make the mystery primary and the game the backdrop the answer races toward. More somber and strange; trades away the high-concept hook up front.
- **C — Two-hander.** Structure explicitly as *Luke's book* and *Ryn's book*, braided at equal weight and converging — almost two novellas in one. Best if Ryn is a true co-protagonist, and it sets up the open-ended sequel most cleanly (Lryn, Luke's school, the unanswered choice).

**Overall recommendation:** the braid above, run with the discipline of shape **A** (stay mostly in the Senate; the off-world strand intercut but lean), POV recommendation #2, single-day-plus-flashback timeline. It keeps the hook, lands the mystery, and converges the mercy and the killing on the same beat.
