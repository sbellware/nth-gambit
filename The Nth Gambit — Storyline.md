# The Nth Gambit — Storyline

*Current overview and structure for the Star Wars fan-fiction draft. Companion to the Manuscript and Universe files. Refreshed to reflect the full development of Ryn, Thrawn, the climax, and the open ending.*

## Timeline placement

An **alternate-universe Galactic Civil War** story occupying the *Return of the Jedi* slot (the deaths of the Emperor and Vader, the war's end) — roughly the **post–*A New Hope*, ~4 ABY** era — but in an AU where the war never ended at Endor and **ground on into a long, decaying age**: strong at the center, fraying at the rim, settled at last by the wager instead of a battle. (Full note in the Universe file.)

## The spine

**One line:** To end a galaxy-spanning war, Luke wagers everything on a single baseball game where the Force has been switched off — and the only way to *win* is to free the tormented creatures doing the switching, a mercy that can only be performed by one broken man whose buried gift turns out to be the unmaking of the Force itself.

**Theme:** the exploited held in thrall by zealots serving the already-gone — and the Force, at the very end, recognizing itself. Nearly every central figure is a broken or enslaved thing: the ysalamiri, Ryn, the clones, even Vader. The story frees each through recognition and *contact*, against two architects of domination who are themselves already dead in their way — the Emperor (who cannot bear to hold a living thing) and Thrawn (literally late, his galaxy-remaking scheme inherited by fools chasing cellulose). Its deepest argument: everything in the book suffers *through* the Force — the ysalamiri, the Jedi, the people, the midichlorians, the Force itself — and the war's true means is the Force, so the only end to the cycle is to *quiet the means*. The unspeakable conclusion the story circles: to end the suffering, the Force must end. And the thing Thrawn tried to **force** could only ever be done **freely**, by someone who loved the galaxy enough to give up the Force rather than wield it. (Held as the submerged thesis — felt, never stated as doctrine.)

## Overview — beginning to end

**The wager.** The Galactic Civil War has dragged on so long no one can pinpoint the moment it began. To end it, Luke strikes a bargain with the Emperor: settle the whole war with one baseball game beneath the dome of the Galactic Senate, loser lays down arms forever. To keep the Force off the field, the diminished Trade Federation — now a stripped-down, monkish arbiter sect — lines the chamber with ysalamiri, creatures that "still" everyone's Force down to a handsbreadth of reach. The coral dust that chalks the baselines is the one material midichlorians cling to even in death — so, quietly, the foul lines are made of the Force. The benches are the soldiers, smugglers, and pilots who learned the game at the front.

**The game.** Stilling doesn't weaken anyone — Vader is still Vader — it only shortens the tether, forcing every play into touching distance. The players assume nine innings will decide it, until they learn a baseball game can't end in a tie. The strategy flips: don't try to win, refuse to lose, hold the deadlock and run the clock — because there's a secret clock only their side is watching.

**The investigation.** Off-world, a Jedi handler — Vela Cas Astyrian, "Val Korr," a survivor of Ki-Adi-Mundi's circle — runs a field operative chasing the ysalamiri's secret: **Ryn Galt**, a Galactic Marine and Clone Wars veteran. The truth: the stilling is a breeding-state effect. In the wild the ysalamiri are tiny golden-jellyfish things that bloom and suppress the Force only briefly, once every couple of years. Grand Admiral **Thrawn** bred them bloated and locked in permanent reproductive torment so it never switches off — not to beat Rebellion or Empire, but to silence the Force *itself*, a hedge against Jedi and Sith alike, born of envy (the gift wasted on amateurs; it should have been *his*). He tried for years to exterminate the midichlorians, failed, and settled for breaking them. He died before realizing any of it; the breeding moon fell to the Trade Federation by accident, swept up in their hunt for cheap, foreclosed, ex-Imperial forest moons.

**Ryn — the hinge.** Ryn was garrisoned and abandoned on that *same* breeding moon, kept at a dead Empire's post by a true-believer sergeant who'd see them all die before admitting the war was lost. He and the ysalamiri suffered on the same poisoned rock, at the same time. He's chosen for the mission because Val Korr has never seen anyone with so little response to the Force — he reads as absence, a hole where a signature should be, like a zombie, present and beside himself at once. The cruel irony she only grasps at the end: she picked him for being Force-*dead*, and he was Force-*full* all along — buried under trauma, inert, like the baselines. His PTSD visions are partly real Force-sight; he can never tell which.

**The climax.** Ryn subs in late at first base — closest to Vader's dugout. Because he's unreadable even on contact, he pulls off the hidden-ball tag that wins the game; no one feels it coming. Vader rejects the wager with violence — vaults the dugout, ignites his bat-saber, swings for Ryn's neck — and Luke's already-lit bat comes across to block. The duel is afoot: igniting bats, then Vader ripping up the burning baselines as jousting lances; the treaty arena becomes a battlefield. As the ysalamiri fade in the long extra innings, three things converge: Ryn, freed by the creatures through a wordless trauma-to-trauma recognition (he hands them back the legacy Thrawn erased; they free his buried Force in return), can now switch the Force on and off — blinking it to dazzle Vader, and, timed to the instant a wielder reaches out, opening a vacuum that tears the Force clean out of them, leaving diatomaceous dust on the skin (becoming, for those seconds, exactly the tormentor Thrawn's breeders were). The worn-down Emperor is ended by all three at once: Ryn snatches the Force out of him, Luke drives the living-wood blade slow and intimate, Vader's hand joins his banked reserve to both. No one could have done it alone.

**The aftermath.** And then, just like that, it's quiet — a wrong, anticlimactic silence. A new era with no fanfare, no cheering, only the dead. Ryn wonders if any of it was worth it, thinks of home and his wife, wonders how a man carries this back to a kitchen table. The old Senate lies in ruins, to be rebuilt: a symbol for the civilization that comes next, neither Republic nor Empire.

**The epilogue.** Home, on a too-short couch, his head in Lryn's lap. Ryn can now turn the Force fully on or off at will — alone among all creatures. Luke's holo-invitation to the new Jedi school waits, unplayed. Luke *knows* he could train Ryn and draw the fuller Force out of him — and that's the danger (the Anakin precedent). But he senses something else in Ryn: not the dark side, a *void* — the still point Luke's chased his whole life and never reached. And he can't tell whether what he feels is fear or *deliverance*: the Force has been turmoil since Ben handed him the saber on Tatooine, and Ryn is the one being who could switch it off and finally set him down. The most dangerous feeling isn't the fear of being unmade — it's the wanting of it. The open question — does Ryn go, or stay on the couch with Lryn and their jizz records — is never answered, because the real answer is heavier than yes or no.

## The braid — three strands

- **Strand A — The Game (Senate, present day).** Close on Luke and the dugout. The wager, the stilling, the tactics, hold-the-tie, the hidden bat-conduit, the twelfth-inning duel. The clock and the spectacle, and the high-concept hook.
- **Strand B — The Investigation (off-world, converging on the present).** Close on Ryn, run by Val Korr over comms (Ryn isn't a Jedi and isn't on the Jedi net; he reports to his handler, who feeds it up the chain). The ysalamiri mystery and Ryn's interiority — the visions, the buried Force he doesn't know he has.
- **Strand C — Flashbacks (deep past, sparing).** Order 66 / Mygeeto (Val Korr's and Ryn's origin wound), soldiers' ball at the front, and Thrawn only ever as discovered records — never voiced. The two flashback veins turn out to be one place: the moon Ryn suffered on is Thrawn's breeding moon, the ysalamiri's farm.

## Timeline — LOCKED: converging timelines

Three time-bands, not two: **deep past** (Clone Wars / Mygeeto / Thrawn's records), **the lead-up** (Ryn abandoned on the moon → the investigation → his journey in), and **the present** (game day, one afternoon, into the twelfth) plus the epilogue.

The game runs real-time. Ryn's strand runs in the lead-up but *catches up* — his intercut scenes start weeks out and march forward until, by the climax, his timeline syncs with game day and he walks into the Senate. Two clocks closing on one point; the moment they sync is his substitution, which is also the climax.

**Split the answer to keep the clock honest:** the *research* (what the ysalamiri are, how to switch them off in principle) finishes in the lead-up — by the time Ryn reaches the Senate the dugout already knows the win is to free the creatures. The thing that only resolves on game day is the *human key*: that it takes the trauma-bond, and that Ryn alone can do it. **The science is antecedent; the mercy is live.** The deep past stays seasoning, dipped into and left. The moon is the secret hinge — its reveal lands as Ryn's timeline catches up to the present, time and place clicking together at once.

## How the climaxes interlock

The duel (Luke/Vader/Emperor) and the ysalamiri thread (Ryn) run **concurrent**, and Ryn's single on/off gift powers the whole convergence: the unreadable hidden-ball tag at first, the Force-blink that dazzles Vader, the carry of ysalamiri to the Imperial dugout, and finally the vacuum-snatch on the worn-down Emperor. At the exact moment Luke performs the intimate *killing*, Ryn performs the intimate *mercy* of freeing the creatures — the book's two hands closing at once. The Force "recognizes itself" twice (Vader feels his son and turns; Ryn and the ysalamiri free each other) — both the precise rebuke to Thrawn's project to abolish the Force.

## Locked decisions

1. **POV — LOCKED.** Dual close-third: Luke for the game, Ryn for the investigation; periodic short dips into a ysalamiri's alien interior; Han / the droid (C-3PO) for texture. **No Thrawn POV.**
2. **Ryn's hidden Force — LOCKED: kept hidden.** His being "unaffected" reads as luck or brokenness; the prose only wonders. His visions are partly true Force-sight and he can never tell which. **Ryn is a switch, not a sword** — his native gift is the *cycling* on/off, not abiding Force power; the latent current is there, and Luke genuinely could draw it out, which is exactly the danger.
3. **Timeline — LOCKED.** Converging timelines, split-answer, the moon as the hinge (see above).
4. **Thrawn — LOCKED: dead and discovered, never voiced.** Records and annotations only; never on-page, not even in memory. No one writes Thrawn's voice but Zahn.
5. **Midichlorian-death lore — LOCKED: narrator musing only.** "Do they gather here to die?" — never confirmed, like the Emperor's vulnerability.

## The deepest reading (held, never declared)

Ryn's purpose may be to **still the Force itself** — the only end to a war whose true means is the Force. To take up the Force would be to join the thing that must be silenced; one day it would mean stilling even Luke. Maybe Luke already knows — and half-wants it (deliverance from a lifetime of turmoil). Keep this as dread and intuition under the open ending; never let a character state it as doctrine. It's why the epilogue can't answer whether Ryn goes: the real answer is heavier than yes or no.

## Alternate shapes (if a different build is wanted)

- **A — Game-pure (novella).** Never leave the Senate; the moon, Thrawn, and Ryn's findings arrive only as comms and flashback. Tighter, more claustrophobic.
- **B — Investigation-forward.** Open cold on Ryn on the moon; the mystery primary, the game the backdrop. More somber; trades away the hook up front.
- **C — Two-hander.** *Luke's book* and *Ryn's book* braided at equal weight and converging — best if Ryn is a true co-protagonist, and it sets up the open-ended sequel most cleanly.

**Working recommendation:** the braid above, run with shape **A**'s discipline (stay mostly in the Senate; the off-world strand intercut but lean), the locked decisions, converging timeline.
