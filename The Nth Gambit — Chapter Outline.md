# The Nth Gambit — Chapter Outline

*Working chapter-by-chapter outline. Companion to the Manuscript, Universe, and Storyline files. Beats are targets, not scripture.*

## Canon vs. our AU — the Trade Federation (read first)

- **Canon:** the Trade Federation was a Neimoidian-led commercial cartel, profit-obsessed, led by a **Viceroy** (Nute Gunray), its fleet built from repurposed **Lucrehulk-class** freighters (the three-kilometer donut-ships, droid armies aboard). It **fell at the end of the Clone Wars** — Gunray killed by Vader on Mustafar (19 BBY), its assets absorbed into the Empire. By the Galactic Civil War, canonically, it no longer exists.
- **Our AU (reconciled with canon — splinter remnants):** canon holds. The Trade Federation *did* fall at the end of the Clone Wars; its leadership was purged and its assets folded into the Empire. **What survived is wreckage** — scattered Neimoidian houses, ships, traders, and trade-routes too far-flung or too minor to be swept up when the cartel died, the ones that "drifted below the cutoff line." Across the decades of the Galactic Civil War, out on the margins, those remnants slowly reconstituted — not as one revived Federation but as a scatter of **splinter sects**, each led by someone styling himself **Viceroy** and laying claim to the dead title (none with the legitimacy of the original). **"The schism," then, is the fragmentation of a corpse** — the scattering and partial reassembly of a dissolved cartel, not the splitting of a living one. **And being true splinters, they diverged.** Cut off from one another, each drifted into its own values and appetites, until they'd grown into things the others would no longer recognize. Most didn't last. **Only two or three sects survive now** — fully disintegrated from each other, no longer in contact, each gone entirely its own way. The **Arbiter Sect** is one of the survivors, defined by its penitent turn: a lower caste among what's left, but not low in the galaxy — it still has ships and markets. Duty-bound now to those ravaged by a war the old Federation did nothing to stop, it has moved into **structural cellulose**, harvesting forest moons to rebuild a galaxy full of refugees. *(This squares with the manuscript prose: "a new monastic order, fallen out the bottom of a diminished Federation, the houses that had drifted below the cutoff line, too near the epicenter when the crash came.")*
- **Lean on canon for:** Neimoidians, Lucrehulk ships, the Viceroy title, the cartel's old reputation for profit and political machination. **Diverge for:** the survival, the schism/six-sect structure, the monkish Arbiter Sect. Keep the old machinator DNA visible under the new piety — diplomacy and maneuvering were always Federation specialties.

## Chapter One — *(working title: "The Arbiters")*

**POV / register:** wry, world-weary omniscient-leaning close third, settling toward Pell.

**Structure:** the chapter's **first movement is the operation + Federation background** — operational detail of harvesting the moon, woven with who the Arbiters are now (the splinter remnant, the penitent-but-still-profiteering sect, the schism, the hyperdrive wandering). The **Leia/Luke dispatch arrives partway through**, turning a routine survey day into the hinge of a war. The cold open already drafted in the manuscript (Pell carrying the dispatch) is the *pivot* of the chapter, not its first page — it should be preceded by the harvest/background material. *(Manuscript note: the current opening runs lean and reaches the dispatch quickly; when drafting, expand the operational + background front-matter ahead of it.)*

**Setting:** a Trade Federation (Arbiter Sect) ship in slow orbit over a forest moon. The moon is **Thrawn's ysalamiri farm**, though no one aboard frames it that way — it's just another green world to be surveyed and stripped.

**Beats:**

1. **Open on the operation — harvesting the moon.** The chapter opens in the **operational detail** of the cellulose harvest, *not* on the dispatch. **And it's no fresh arrival:** the *Restitution* was *assigned* this moon and has worked it for **years** — a settled, long-running strip-harvest, the crew steeped in the place, the routines worn smooth. Establish the rhythm of the work: the survey of the canopy for the densest stands, the cutting and extraction, the processing of raw growth into engineered **structural cellulose**, the tallying of tonnage into the holds — the slow, patient labor the Arbiters have made their penance. Green world filling the viewport; the galaxy is *littered* with forest moons, and this is just one more. **The quiet irony to plant operationally:** they are stripping *Thrawn's ysalamiri farm* and don't frame it that way — and the crew working near the moon carries a low, grinding fatigue they all chalk up to long tours and hard duty, never guessing it's the creatures below them taxing every body within reach (the residual-midichlorian drain; ties to the "stilling taxes everyone" note). Name nothing; let it be ambient and wrong.
2. **Who the Arbiters are now.** A lower caste within the Federation since the schism — but not low in the galaxy; they have ships and markets. What sets them apart is a sense of *duty* to those ravaged by a war the Federation did nothing to stop. They've gone more monk than merchant. (Texture: never hear the word *profit* aboard; only salvage, tonnage, the patient work of rebuilding.)
3. **The schism, briefly.** Six sects, six Viceroys. Not all can claim to be the apex leader; some are plainly stronger than others — but the differences are negligible. (Don't over-explain; a paragraph of weary background.)
4. **More Federation background** (canon-grounded; see top): Neimoidian stock, the old profit-cartel reputation, the Lucrehulk bones of the ship, diplomacy and machination as ancestral specialties — the machinator DNA still there under the new piety.
5. **The operation, and the moon's secret.** They're extracting structural cellulose from this forest moon — and it's Thrawn's ysalamiri farm. **They acquired the moon without first understanding what they had.** But it wasn't lost on the sect that they now possessed the power to **still Force users**. (Plant it quietly; the reader may not yet grasp the weight.)
6. **Why the stilling suits them — and how it keeps them hidden.** The Arbiter Sect wants nothing more to do with the wars between the Alliance and the Empire — that's where they *used* to be; now they want their new mission. And the moon is more than a cellulose claim: working it for years, they have lived inside its **Force-insulation** — no Jedi, no Sith, no Force-wielder can sense or reach them here. *This is precisely how the Arbiters have stayed clear of the galaxy's machinations:* parked on a Force-null rock, off everyone's senses, **unfindable** by the only powers that could compel them. The détente isn't only the Rebels and the Empire agreeing to leave them be — it's the Arbiters making themselves impossible to lean on. *But old favors can still be called in.* Diplomacy was always a Federation specialty — even when most of the effort went into political machination.
7. **The contact — through Leia.** The reach-out comes via **Leia Organa**, Luke's connection to the Arbiters: she's deep in the galaxy's refugee crisis and knows the landscape of actors in that space — including the Arbiter Sect, who supply the cellulose for refugee shelter. Likely **Leia herself hails the *Restitution*** to route Luke's request through them, asking the sect to **arbitrate the final conflict** between the Alliance and the Empire — exactly the involvement the Arbiters have been assiduously avoiding. (The dramatized dispatch beat: Pell carries the communique; the senior officer asks what it says; "a request for parlay… peace talks." **Decided:** the dispatch is **routed through Organa's relief office** — Leia's standing relationship with the Arbiters (their cellulose buyer) is the channel that gets it through and makes them answer; the *sender* is Luke. The relief office is a familiar, trusted line to them, which is exactly why they don't just ignore it.)
8. **The offer they can't refuse.** It could mean the **end of the war** — and the end of war is good for business when you're in the structural-cellulose trade and the galaxy is full of unhomed refugees. The commander's instinct is to wash their hands of it (transmit it on to Coruscant, done their part) — but the hook is set: arbitrating *is* the thing they can't, finally, refuse.
9. **Button.** End on the ominous note already drafted — none of them grasping what they're carrying, nor what waits "patient and uncountable, in the green dark of the moon below."

**To weave in (light, not lectures):** the galaxy-is-full-of-forest-moons cosmology (the green worlds get the visitors; the rock-and-ice worlds burn out unseen); the structural-cellulose terminology rule (full term on first use, then "cellulose," sometimes "wood").

## Cast & details for Chapter One — SET

- **The ship: the *Restitution*** — a repurposed Lucrehulk, renamed by the penitent sect; the name signals their atoning mission, ironic against the donut-shaped profit-engine it used to be.
- **The commander: Voss Dorrun** — Neimoidian, weary, pragmatic, duty-bound but tired; wants to do his bit and get back to the survey. Feels Luke's offer as a nuisance first, a hook second.
- **Second-in-command: Sub-Arbiter Tann Reo** — the devout foil; a true believer in the sect's penance who feels the pull of Luke's offer as a moral *duty* where Dorrun feels only the bother.
- **Pell Naru — command-deck signals officer.** Junior; the one who receives and carries dispatches (fits the cold open), with quiet access to the ship's comms and operations. Competent and capable, but *often underestimated* — for his youth, his wispy frame, and the unreadable quiet the older officers gave up trying to parse. His competence is real; people just keep failing to notice it — which later makes him exactly the one who can quietly get Ryn onto the moon.
